Essential AI Terminology

🧠 Artificial Intelligence (AI)
A broad field of computer science focused on creating systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as problem-solving, decision-making, and understanding language. Think of it as the ultimate goal of making computers "smart."
📊 Machine Learning (ML)
A subset of AI. Instead of being explicitly programmed with rules, ML systems learn from data. The more data you give them, the better they get at recognizing patterns and making predictions.
🔗 Deep Learning
A more specialized subset of ML. It uses a specific type of algorithm called a **neural network** to learn from large amounts of data. This is what powers many of the most advanced AI applications today, like self-driving cars and image recognition.
🕸️ Neural Network
Inspired by the human brain, a neural network is a system of interconnected nodes (or "neurons") organized in layers. It learns by finding relationships between data inputs and outputs. The "deep" in deep learning refers to the use of many hidden layers.
👁️ Computer Vision
An AI field that allows computers to "see" and interpret visual information from images and videos. It is used for things like facial recognition and object detection.
💬 Natural Language Processing (NLP)
An AI field that enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language. This is what powers virtual assistants, language translators, and chatbots.
🎯 Model
The core output of a machine learning process. It's the "brain" that has been trained on data to make predictions or decisions. For example, a fraud detection model is trained to predict whether a transaction is fraudulent or not.
⚙️ Algorithm
A set of step-by-step instructions or rules that an AI model uses to perform a task. It's the recipe that the model follows.
🏋️ Training and Inferencing

Training: The process of feeding data into an algorithm to create an AI model. This is where the model learns from historical examples.

Inferencing (or Prediction): The process of using a trained model to make a prediction on new, unseen data.

⚖️ Bias & Fairness

Bias: A systematic error in an AI model that causes it to favor a certain outcome or group over others. This often happens if the training data is not representative or is skewed.

Fairness: The principle of ensuring that an AI system performs justly and without bias for all individuals and groups.

🔧 Fit

Underfitting: The model is too simple and cannot capture the patterns in the training data. It performs poorly on both training and new data.

Overfitting: The model has learned the training data too well, including its random noise. It performs very well on the training data but fails on new data.

🎪 Large Language Model (LLM)
A type of AI model that is trained on a massive amount of text and code. They are designed to understand and generate human-like language for a wide range of tasks, from writing essays to answering questions.

Types of AI Inference

Batch Inferencing

What it is: Running predictions on a large group of data all at once, on a scheduled basis. This is useful for tasks that don't need immediate results, like analyzing monthly sales data or generating reports.

Real-time Inferencing

What it is: Making predictions on single pieces of data as they arrive, with very low delay. This is critical for applications where immediate action is needed, like detecting fraud as a credit card transaction happens or recommending a product to a customer in the moment.

Types of Data in AI

🏷️
Labeled Data
Has a "tag" or "answer" attached to it. For example, an image of a dog labeled "dog." Used for supervised learning.
❓
Unlabeled Data
Has no tags. For example, a large collection of text documents without any descriptions. Used for unsupervised learning.
📊
Tabular Data
Data organized into rows and columns, like a spreadsheet.
📈
Time-series Data
Data collected over a period of time, such as stock prices or sensor readings.
🖼️
Image Data
Visual information.
📝
Text Data
Language-based information.
🏗️
Structured Data
Data that is well-organized and fits into a fixed format (e.g., a database table).
🌊
Unstructured Data
Data that does not have a predefined format (e.g., text from emails, social media posts, or audio files).

Types of Machine Learning

👨‍🏫 Supervised Learning
The model is trained on **labeled data** with the goal of learning a mapping from input to output. It's like having a teacher who provides both the question and the correct answer.
Example: Spam detection, image classification.
🔍 Unsupervised Learning
The model is given **unlabeled data** and must find patterns or structure on its own. There is no "correct answer." It's like finding a way to group similar items without knowing what those groups are beforehand.
Example: Customer segmentation, anomaly detection.
🎮 Reinforcement Learning
The model learns through trial and error by interacting with an environment. It receives rewards for good actions and penalties for bad ones, with the goal of maximizing its total reward over time. Think of training a dog with treats.
Example: Training a robot to walk, or an AI to play a game.

Key Insight

Think of AI like learning to drive: AI is the goal (becoming a good driver), Machine Learning is the method (practicing with an instructor), and Deep Learning is like having multiple instructors teaching different aspects simultaneously (parking, highway driving, city navigation) until you master complex driving scenarios.
